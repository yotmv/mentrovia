> This is general small business guidance, not legal, tax, payroll, or accounting advice. Verify requirements with the appropriate government agency and review decisions with a qualified professional before filing or relying on them.

If you sell goods — and for many kinds of services — in Texas, sales tax is likely part of your life. The permit is free, but the obligations that come with it are ongoing.

## Who generally needs a permit

You generally need a Texas sales tax permit **before** making taxable sales if you are engaged in business in Texas and you sell or lease tangible personal property, or sell a taxable service. Key points:

- **Goods:** most retail sales of physical products are taxable.
- **Services:** Texas taxes specific categories of services defined by statute — the Comptroller publishes the list. Many services are not taxable, but don't guess: check the taxable services publication or ask the Comptroller.
- **Online and marketplace sales:** selling through your own site or a marketplace does not automatically exempt you; marketplace rules have their own wrinkles worth confirming.

Mentrovia deliberately does not decide taxability for you. Treat your situation as **likely taxable**, **possibly taxable**, or **needs confirmation** until the Comptroller or a CPA confirms it.

## What having a permit means day to day

- **Collect** the correct combined state and local rate on taxable sales — current rates are published by the Comptroller; don't rely on remembered numbers.
- **Keep sales tax out of your revenue.** Tax you collect is the state's money held in trust. Track it separately in your books (a liability, not income) and ideally park it in a separate savings account.
- **File and pay on schedule.** The Comptroller assigns a filing frequency (such as monthly, quarterly, or yearly) based on your volume. A return is generally due even for periods with zero sales.
- **Keep records** of sales, exemption certificates, and returns.

## Common mistakes

- Making taxable sales before holding a permit, then facing back taxes and penalties.
- Spending collected sales tax as if it were revenue.
- Assuming a service is non-taxable without checking the statute's list.
- Ignoring local rate differences between the cities and counties where you deliver or perform work.

## Questions to ask a professional

- Are my specific products or services taxable in Texas?
- How should marketplace or out-of-city sales be handled?
- How do I set up my invoices and bookkeeping so collected tax stays separated?
