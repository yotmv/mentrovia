> This is general small business guidance, not legal, tax, payroll, or accounting advice. Verify requirements with the appropriate government agency and review decisions with a qualified professional before filing or relying on them.

Contractors are simpler than employees — no payroll taxes, no withholding — but they come with their own paperwork, and one big trap.

## The trap first: classification

Calling someone a contractor doesn't make them one. The IRS looks at the actual relationship — behavioral control (who directs how the work is done), financial control (whose tools, whose expenses, opportunity for profit/loss), and the relationship's nature (permanency, benefits, how central the work is to your business). If you set their hours, provide the tools, and they work only for you indefinitely, you likely have an employee regardless of the contract's label. Misclassification means back payroll taxes and penalties. When in doubt, get a professional opinion before the relationship starts.

## Contractor onboarding checklist

- **Collect a W-9 before the first payment.** It gives you their legal name, entity type, and taxpayer ID — everything January's filings need. Chasing W-9s in January from contractors you no longer use is miserable; make it a precondition of payment.
- A simple written agreement: scope, price, deadlines, ownership of work product, and independence terms.
- Certificate of insurance where relevant (trades especially).

## Tracking through the year

- Record every contractor payment **by payee** in your books, with a category like "contract labor."
- Note the payment method: payments made by credit card or third-party payment platforms are generally reported by the processor, not by you — cash, check, and bank transfers are generally yours to report. Confirm current rules with your CPA at year end.
- Keep W-9s together (a single folder, physical or digital).

## Year-end

Businesses generally file **Form 1099-NEC** for each unincorporated contractor paid at or above the reporting threshold during the year (check the current threshold and due date with the IRS — the deadline lands early in the new year). Copies go to the contractor and the IRS. Your bookkeeping software or CPA can generate them from clean payee records in minutes.

## Questions to ask a professional

- Do my contractors pass the classification tests, honestly applied?
- Which of my payees need 1099s this year given how I paid them?
