The Texas franchise tax is a privilege tax on entities formed or doing business in Texas. It surprises many new LLC owners because it exists even though Texas has no personal income tax — and because a report can be required even when **no tax is owed**.

## Who it generally applies to

Taxable entities generally include LLCs, corporations, limited partnerships, professional associations, and similar formal entities formed in or doing business in Texas. Sole proprietorships and certain general partnerships owned entirely by natural persons are generally **not** taxable entities. If you formed an entity with the Secretary of State, assume franchise tax reporting applies to you until confirmed otherwise.

## How it works at a high level

- The tax is based on an entity's **margin**, calculated from total revenue under rules the Comptroller publishes.
- There is a **no-tax-due revenue threshold**, adjusted over time. Entities at or below it owe no tax — but reporting or information filings can still be required. Check the current threshold on the Comptroller's site rather than relying on any remembered figure.
- **Annual reports are due each year** on a date set by the Comptroller, with the first report timing driven by your formation date. Confirm the current due date on the Comptroller's franchise tax page.
- Alongside the tax report, most entities also file a **Public Information Report (PIR)** or **Ownership Information Report (OIR)** keeping ownership and management details current.

## Why it matters even when you owe nothing

Missing franchise tax filings can cost an entity its right to transact business in Texas and, eventually, its charter — which can strip away the liability protection you formed the entity for. The fix is usually straightforward (file the overdue reports), but prevention is cheaper.

## What to track during the year

- Total revenue, kept current in your bookkeeping — it drives everything.
- Your formation anniversary and the annual report deadline.
- Any Comptroller correspondence — webfile numbers and notices matter at filing time.

## Questions to ask a professional

- Is my entity a taxable entity, and when is my first report due?
- Am I under the current no-tax-due threshold, and what do I still have to file if so?
- Which margin calculation method would minimize my tax if I'm over the threshold?
