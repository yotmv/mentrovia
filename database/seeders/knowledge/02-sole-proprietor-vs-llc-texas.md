The most common early decision for a Texas owner-operator is whether to stay a sole proprietor or form an LLC. Neither is automatically right — they trade off cost, paperwork, liability, and tax flexibility.

## Sole proprietorship

You and the business are legally the same person. There is nothing to file with the state to *be* a sole proprietor, though operating under a business name usually means filing an assumed name certificate with your county.

**Typical advantages:** free to start, minimal paperwork, simplest possible taxes (business results flow onto your personal return).

**Typical trade-offs:** no liability separation — business debts and lawsuits reach your personal assets; harder to open business credit; the business ends with you.

## Texas LLC

A limited liability company is a separate legal entity formed by filing a certificate of formation with the Texas Secretary of State and paying the state filing fee.

**Typical advantages:** liability separation between business and personal assets (when the entity is respected and finances stay separate); flexible federal tax treatment — a single-member LLC is taxed like a sole proprietorship by default but can elect other treatment; more credibility with banks, landlords, and customers.

**Typical trade-offs:** formation and ongoing costs; a Texas franchise tax reporting obligation applies to taxable entities even in years when no tax is due; more formality — you need a registered agent, and you should adopt an operating agreement and keep finances strictly separate.

## The part people miss

An LLC only protects you if you treat it as a real, separate business. Running LLC income through a personal bank account, signing contracts in your own name, or skipping required state filings can undermine the separation you formed it for. Liability protection is also never absolute — it does not shield you from your own professional negligence or personal guarantees.

## Questions to ask a professional

- Given my industry's risks, how much does liability separation matter for me?
- What would an LLC cost me per year in Texas, all-in, including franchise tax reporting?
- Should the LLC keep its default tax treatment or elect something else?
- Do I need an operating agreement even as a single member? (Usually a good idea.)
