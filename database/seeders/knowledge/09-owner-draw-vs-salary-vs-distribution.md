> This is general small business guidance, not legal, tax, payroll, or accounting advice. Verify requirements with the appropriate government agency and review decisions with a qualified professional before filing or relying on them.

How you pay yourself is not a style choice — it's dictated mostly by your entity type and federal tax classification. Using the wrong method can create tax problems; using the right one badly can starve the business of cash.

## The main methods

**Owner draw.** Taking money out of the business for personal use. This is the normal method for sole proprietors and single-member LLCs with default tax treatment. A draw is **not** a deductible expense and **not** a paycheck — you're taxed on the business's profit regardless of what you draw, generally including self-employment tax, usually paid via quarterly estimated taxes.

**W-2 salary.** Being on the business's payroll like any employee, with withholding and payroll taxes. Generally required for shareholder-employees of S corporations (reasonable compensation) and standard for C corporation owner-officers. Sole proprietors and partners generally **cannot** put themselves on W-2 payroll for their own business.

**Distribution.** A payout of profits to owners of an S corporation (or profit allocation in a partnership/multi-member LLC). S corporation distributions are generally not subject to payroll taxes — which is exactly why the IRS insists on reasonable W-2 wages *first* for owner-employees who work in the business.

**Guaranteed payments.** Partnership-world's cousin of a salary — fixed payments to a partner for services regardless of profit. See the partnership article.

## Quick mapping by structure

| Structure | Typical owner pay |
|---|---|
| Sole proprietor / single-member LLC (default) | Owner draws + quarterly estimated taxes |
| Partnership / multi-member LLC (default) | Draws against profit share; guaranteed payments for services |
| S corporation | Reasonable W-2 salary first, then distributions |
| C corporation | W-2 salary; dividends are possible but double-taxed |

## Cash-flow policy that works

Whatever the method, make it **deliberate and recorded**: a consistent amount on a schedule, recorded as draws/payroll/distributions in the books — not ad-hoc transfers when the personal account runs low. Keep a tax reserve so the estimated-tax bill never surprises you.

## Questions to ask a CPA

- What is my entity's tax classification right now, and what pay methods does it allow?
- How much should I set aside for taxes per dollar drawn?
- Would an S election reduce my overall tax at my profit level, considering payroll costs it would add?
