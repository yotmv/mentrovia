> This is general small business guidance, not legal, tax, payroll, or accounting advice. Verify requirements with the appropriate government agency and review decisions with a qualified professional before filing or relying on them.

A brand kit is the small set of decisions that make your business look consistent everywhere: name, tagline, voice, colors, type, and a logo. For a new small business, done-and-consistent beats polished-and-someday.

## The seven pieces

1. **Name.** Say-able on the phone, spellable after hearing it, and available — check the Secretary of State's records, county assumed-name records, domain, and social handles before falling in love. (A conceptual name check is not a trademark search; if the name is commercially important, that's an attorney conversation.)
2. **Tagline.** One sentence: what you do, for whom, and why you're the pick. "Same-day mobile mower repair in Travis County" beats "Quality you can trust."
3. **Positioning.** One paragraph, internal-only: who exactly you serve, the alternative they'd otherwise choose, and the one reason you win. Every ad you write later gets easier.
4. **Voice.** Three adjectives you are ("plain-spoken, prompt, local") and one you're not ("never corporate"). Apply to every caption and email.
5. **Colors.** One primary, one accent, one neutral. Write down the hex codes and reuse them everywhere — consistency, not artistry, is the goal.
6. **Type.** One font for headings, one for body. Free, widely available fonts are fine.
7. **Logo.** A clean wordmark (your name, your font, your color) is a legitimate starter logo. Ensure it survives being small, one-color, and on a dark background.

## The customer persona (15 minutes)

Write one paragraph about one real-feeling customer: who they are, what triggered the need, where they looked, what they feared, why they chose you. Marketing copy written *to that person* consistently outperforms copy written to "everyone."

## Package it

Put it all on one page — name, tagline, positioning, voice words, hex codes, fonts, logo files, persona — and reuse ruthlessly: social bios, invoices, truck signage, ads. Update yearly (see the yearly checklist), not weekly.
