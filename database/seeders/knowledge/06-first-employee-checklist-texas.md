> This is general small business guidance, not legal, tax, payroll, or accounting advice. Verify requirements with the appropriate government agency and review decisions with a qualified professional before filing or relying on them.

Hiring your first employee changes your compliance picture more than any other single step. Here is the typical Texas checklist.

## Before the first day

- **Get an EIN** if you don't have one — payroll filings require it.
- **Confirm they're actually an employee.** If you're treating someone as a contractor to avoid payroll, read the worker classification article first; misclassification is one of the most expensive small business mistakes.
- **Register with the Texas Workforce Commission.** Texas employers generally must register for a state unemployment tax account promptly after becoming liable — the TWC's site explains liability and registration.
- **Decide on workers' compensation.** Texas does not require most private employers to carry workers' comp, but going without ("non-subscribing") has notice requirements and exposes you to lawsuits that coverage would bar. This is a real decision — review it with an insurance agent or attorney, not a default.
- **Choose a payroll provider.** Federal withholding, Social Security and Medicare taxes, federal unemployment tax, deposit schedules, and quarterly/annual filings are unforgiving of DIY errors. A provider automates nearly all of it.

## At hire

- **Form W-4** — federal withholding elections.
- **Form I-9** — employment eligibility verification, with documents examined on schedule.
- **New hire reporting** — Texas employers report new hires to the state's new hire registry (operated by the Office of the Attorney General) shortly after the start date; most payroll providers handle this.
- Direct deposit authorization, emergency contacts, and any policy acknowledgments or handbook.

## Ongoing

- Run payroll on a consistent schedule and keep pay stubs and time records — the Texas Payday Law governs how and when employees must be paid.
- Pay attention to minimum wage and overtime rules (federal FLSA and Texas wage-and-hour law).
- File state unemployment reports (TWC) and federal payroll filings on schedule — your provider should do both.
- Budget the true cost: wages plus employer taxes, insurance, and any benefits.

## Questions to ask a professional

- Is this worker an employee or a contractor under the actual tests?
- Should I subscribe to workers' compensation given my industry's risk?
- Which payroll provider fits my size, and what does it not handle that I still own?
