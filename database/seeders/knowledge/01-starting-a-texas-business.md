> This is general small business guidance, not legal, tax, payroll, or accounting advice. Verify requirements with the appropriate government agency and review decisions with a qualified professional before filing or relying on them.

Starting a business in Texas is less about one big filing and more about getting a handful of foundations in the right order. Here is the typical sequence.

## 1. Settle on what you sell and what you'll call it

Before any paperwork, be able to describe what you sell, to whom, and under what name. Your business name flows into every later step — entity filings, assumed name certificates, your bank account, and permits — so decide it early and check that it isn't already taken. The Texas Secretary of State's records and a basic web search are good first checks; a trademark search is a deeper step to discuss with an attorney if the name matters commercially.

## 2. Choose a legal structure

Texas businesses commonly operate as sole proprietorships, partnerships, LLCs, or corporations. The choice affects personal liability, taxes, cost, and paperwork. The Texas Secretary of State publishes a plain-English comparison, and the IRS explains the federal tax treatment of each form. This is the decision most worth reviewing with an attorney or CPA before you commit — see the companion article on sole proprietorship vs. LLC.

## 3. Make the required filings

- **Formal entities** (LLCs, corporations, limited partnerships) file a certificate of formation with the Texas Secretary of State.
- **Sole proprietors and general partnerships** operating under a name other than the owner's legal name typically file an assumed name certificate at the county level.
- Some industries also need professional or local licenses — Texas has no single general business license, so check your city, county, and industry regulator.

## 4. Get your tax identifiers

Most businesses benefit from a free federal EIN from the IRS, and any business selling taxable goods or services in Texas needs a sales tax permit from the Texas Comptroller before making taxable sales.

## 5. Separate your money

Open a dedicated business bank account and set up basic bookkeeping before revenue starts flowing. Both are dramatically easier to do now than to untangle later.

## Questions to ask a professional

- Which structure fits my liability exposure and tax situation?
- Does my industry or city require licenses or permits?
- When should I register for sales tax, and are my sales taxable at all?
