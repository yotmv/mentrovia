> This is general small business guidance, not legal, tax, payroll, or accounting advice. Verify requirements with the appropriate government agency and review decisions with a qualified professional before filing or relying on them.

Once a quarter, zoom out from the weekly and monthly rhythm and look at taxes and trajectory.

## Federal estimated taxes

Owners of pass-through businesses (sole proprietors, partners, most LLC members, S corporation shareholders on distributions) generally pay tax through **quarterly estimated payments** — the IRS publishes the payment schedule and safe-harbor rules. Each quarter:

- Compare actual year-to-date profit against the assumption behind your payments.
- Adjust the next payment if profit is running well above or below plan.
- Confirm the payment actually went out (and was recorded).

If you're on payroll (S corporation salary), check that withholding is tracking your total expected liability.

## State filings

- **Sales tax:** quarterly filers — file and pay by the due date. All permit holders: does the liability in your books match the reserve account?
- **Franchise tax:** once a year the report is due, but quarterly is when you check the revenue trajectory against the current no-tax-due threshold (on the Comptroller's site) so the filing season holds no surprises.

## Payroll (if applicable)

- Quarterly federal payroll filings and TWC unemployment reports — confirm your provider filed them and the amounts match your records.

## Money decisions

- **Owner pay review:** is your draw/salary level still right for profit and cash?
- **Contractor totals:** year-to-date by payee — anyone crossing the 1099 threshold you haven't collected a W-9 from?
- **Cash-flow forecast:** next quarter's known obligations (taxes, insurance renewals, seasonal dips) against expected cash.
- **Pricing and margins:** one honest look per quarter beats a crisis reprice every two years.

## The CPA touchpoint

A short quarterly call with your CPA or bookkeeper — profit trend, estimated payments, anything unusual — is the cheapest tax planning that exists. Year-end-only relationships mostly file history; quarterly relationships change outcomes.
