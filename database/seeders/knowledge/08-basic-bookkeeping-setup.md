> This is general small business guidance, not legal, tax, payroll, or accounting advice. Verify requirements with the appropriate government agency and review decisions with a qualified professional before filing or relying on them.

Bookkeeping is just the habit of recording what the business earns and spends, in categories that make taxes and decisions easy. Set it up before you need it.

## The minimum viable setup

1. **A dedicated business bank account** — the foundation everything else builds on.
2. **Bookkeeping software** — any mainstream small-business product that connects to your bank feed. The category matters more than the brand; pick one you'll actually open weekly.
3. **A starter chart of accounts** — the category list for your money. A service business can start with something like: sales income; sales tax collected (a liability, not income); contractor payments; supplies and materials; software subscriptions; insurance; vehicle/mileage; advertising; bank and processing fees; owner contributions; owner draws.
4. **A receipt habit** — photograph receipts as they happen and attach them to transactions. The IRS's recordkeeping guidance explains what to keep and why; the practical answer is "receipts, invoices, bank statements, and filed returns, organized enough to find."

## Habits that keep books clean

- **Weekly (15 minutes):** categorize new transactions, attach receipts, glance at cash.
- **Monthly:** reconcile the bank account against the books, review your profit & loss, move your tax-reserve percentage.
- **Distinguish the three kinds of money leaving:** expenses (deductible costs), owner draws (not expenses), and sales tax remittances (paying back money you held in trust). Confusing these is the most common small-business books error.
- **Track contractor payments by payee** so January's 1099 work is a report, not an archaeology project.

## Cash vs. accrual, briefly

Cash-basis books record income when money arrives and expenses when paid — simplest, and what most very small businesses use. Accrual records when earned/incurred. Your CPA may prefer one for tax purposes; ask before you're locked into habits.

## When to get help

If reconciling takes hours, revenue is growing, or you're behind more than a quarter — hand the books to a bookkeeper and keep your time for the business. See the article on when to hire professionals.
