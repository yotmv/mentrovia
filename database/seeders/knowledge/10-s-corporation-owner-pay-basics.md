First, a Texas-specific clarification: an "S corporation" is a **federal tax election**, not a Texas entity type. A Texas LLC or corporation *elects* S treatment with the IRS. For Texas franchise tax purposes, the entity is still a taxable entity regardless of its federal election.

## The core rule: reasonable compensation first

If you own an S corporation and work in the business, the IRS expects the company to pay you a **reasonable W-2 salary** for your services **before** you take profit distributions. Salary carries payroll taxes; distributions generally don't — which is the whole appeal, and exactly why the IRS scrutinizes owners who take large distributions on tiny (or zero) salaries. Reclassification of distributions as wages, plus penalties, is the standard outcome of getting this wrong.

"Reasonable" is a facts-and-circumstances judgment: what would you pay someone else to do your job? Factors include your duties, time committed, skill, comparable market salaries, and company size. Your CPA can help document a defensible number.

## What owner pay looks like in practice

1. **W-2 payroll** for yourself on a regular schedule, through a payroll provider (the corporation now has an employee: you).
2. **Distributions** of remaining profit, typically taken periodically and proportional to ownership.
3. Health insurance premiums for a more-than-two-percent shareholder-employee have special W-2 reporting treatment — a classic year-end CPA cleanup item worth getting right during the year.

## Trade-offs to keep in view

- Payroll costs money: provider fees, employer payroll taxes, and filings — the S election only wins when the payroll-tax savings on distributions exceed these costs.
- The salary must be funded even in lean quarters; distributions can flex, wages shouldn't.
- Basis and distribution rules can make cash you take non-tax-free in some situations — track basis with your CPA.

## Questions to ask a CPA

- What salary is defensible as "reasonable" for my role and hours?
- At my profit level, does the S election actually save money after payroll costs?
- How should my health insurance premiums be reported?
- Am I tracking shareholder basis correctly?
