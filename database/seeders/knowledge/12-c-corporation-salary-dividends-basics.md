> This is general small business guidance, not legal, tax, payroll, or accounting advice. Verify requirements with the appropriate government agency and review decisions with a qualified professional before filing or relying on them.

A C corporation is its own taxpayer: it pays corporate income tax on profits, and shareholders pay tax again on dividends they receive. That "double taxation" shapes every owner-pay decision.

## The two main channels

**W-2 salary.** An owner who works in the business is an employee and is paid through payroll with normal withholding. Salary is deductible to the corporation, so it's taxed once (to you). For working owners of small C corporations, salary is usually the primary channel. Note the mirror-image of the S corporation issue: because salary avoids the corporate-level tax, the IRS watches for *unreasonably high* owner salaries in C corporations, not just unreasonably low ones.

**Dividends.** Distributions of after-tax corporate profit to shareholders. Not deductible to the corporation — this is where double taxation bites: the corporation paid tax on the profit, and you pay tax on the dividend. Dividends make sense for returning profit to non-working shareholders or when retained profits are being distributed deliberately.

## The third option: leave it in

C corporations can **retain earnings** to fund growth, taxed only at the corporate level for now. That's a legitimate strategy with limits — accumulating earnings well beyond the business's needs can attract an IRS accumulated-earnings problem. Growth plans and documentation matter.

## Other pieces worth knowing

- **Reimbursements under an accountable plan** let the corporation repay your documented business expenses tax-free — set the plan up properly.
- **Fringe benefits** (health insurance and others) are an area where C corporations have real advantages — a CPA conversation, not a DIY one.
- **Loans to/from shareholders** must be real loans, documented with terms and interest, or they risk being recharacterized as dividends or wages.

## Questions to ask a CPA

- What salary level is defensible for my role — and is any part at risk of being called excessive?
- Should this year's profit be salary, dividend, or retained for growth?
- Would an accountable plan and formal fringe-benefit setup save us money?
- Given my situation, is C status even the right election going forward?
