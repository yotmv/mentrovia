"DBA" (doing business as) and "assumed name" mean the same thing in Texas: operating a business under a name that isn't your legal name (for individuals) or the entity's registered name (for LLCs and corporations).

## When an assumed name filing typically applies

- A **sole proprietor or general partnership** operating under anything other than the owners' surnames — for example, Jane Smith running "Bluebonnet Lawn Care" — generally files an assumed name certificate **with the county clerk** in the counties where business is conducted.
- A **registered entity** (LLC, corporation, limited partnership) operating under a name different from its certificate of formation generally files an assumed name certificate **with the Texas Secretary of State**.

Where you file depends on your entity type and where you operate, and county-level requirements can vary — confirm the specifics with your county clerk or the Secretary of State before relying on a single filing.

## What an assumed name filing does — and doesn't do

An assumed name certificate puts your trade name on public record so customers and creditors can find out who is behind it. It does **not**:

- Create a separate legal entity or any liability protection.
- Reserve or protect the name — someone else can file the same assumed name. Name protection comes from entity registration and trademark law, not DBA filings.
- Replace entity formation, tax registration, or licenses.

## Practical notes

- Certificates are valid for a term of years and must be renewed; set a reminder when you file.
- If your information changes materially (owners, address, entity type), a new filing is generally needed.
- Banks usually ask for your assumed name certificate before opening an account in the trade name.

## Questions to ask a professional

- Do I file with the county, the Secretary of State, or both, given my structure and operating area?
- Should I be protecting this name with an entity registration or trademark instead of relying on the DBA?
